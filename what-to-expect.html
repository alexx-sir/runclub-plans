What toExpect

The first few weeks will feel wrong. That's not a sign the method is failing — it's a sign it's working. Here's exactly what's coming and why.

01
Weeks 1–3 — the hardest part
It's going to feel insultingly slow

Here is what nobody tells you about starting MAF training: for the first 2–4 weeks, you will have to run slower than feels reasonable. Some people have to shuffle. Some have to walk significant portions of the run just to keep their heart rate under their MAF number. This is not a failure. This is the most important diagnostic the method gives you.

If you have to slow way down to stay in Zone 2, it means your aerobic base — your mitochondria, your cardiac output, your fat-burning engine — is underdeveloped. That underdevelopment is exactly why you've been getting winded. The shuffling pace is the starting line, not the finish line.

If you have to slow down more than you expected, that's not you failing the plan. That's the plan diagnosing your aerobic fitness accurately — probably for the first time.
Floris Gierman — Extramilest Method

Most runners who get winded have been training in the "grey zone" for years — too fast to build aerobic base, too slow to build real speed. The MAF method takes you out of the grey zone entirely. The first weeks are uncomfortable because you're running far slower than you're used to. Stick with it.

🐢
Having to walk to stay under MAF Normal
Walk breaks are Zone 2. Brisk walking keeps you in the right zone. You are still training effectively. Within 4–6 weeks, the same effort will carry you further without the walks.
🤏
Feeling like you could go much faster Normal
Zone 2 should feel almost too easy. If it feels easy, you're doing it right. The difficulty is mental — trusting that slow is building something real.
😤
Frustration with the pace Normal
Every intermediate runner feels this. Floris warns about it explicitly. The frustration usually breaks around week 4–5 when you notice your easy pace has quietly improved. Wait for that moment.
💨
Still getting winded in week 1–2 Normal
Your aerobic system takes 3–6 weeks to begin adapting. Keep your heart rate under MAF. The breathing will come.
🚨
Sharp pain in joints or muscles See a physio
General fatigue and muscle soreness are fine. Sharp, localised pain in knees, ankles, or hips is not normal. Don't run through sharp pain. Take an extra rest day and get it looked at.
02
The honest aerobic timeline
When things start to change

The 8-week plan gets you to a 5K finish line. But the aerobic base it starts building is something that compounds for months and years. Here's what the real timeline looks like — so you don't expect too much too soon, and don't quit right before the breakthrough.

W1–2
Weeks 1–2
The humbling
Your MAF pace is probably slower than expected. Walks are frequent. Your legs feel fine but your heart rate climbs fast. This is normal. Your cardiovascular system hasn't caught up to your legs yet. Stay patient.
W3–4
Weeks 3–4
Small signs of life
The walks get shorter. You might go 10–15 minutes without needing one. Your resting heart rate may start to drop slightly. The mitochondria are starting to multiply. Not dramatic yet — but something is shifting.
W5–6
Weeks 5–6
The first click
Most runners have a moment around week 5 where something feels different — the same effort covers more ground. The talk test suddenly feels easy. This is your aerobic engine turning over for the first time. It's small, but it's real progress.
W7–8
Weeks 7–8 — Race Week
You can feel the base
Your easy pace has improved. The club run feels manageable. You cross the 5K finish line breathing — not gasping. That's the whole point. Eight weeks of patience paid off in a single moment.
M2–3
Months 2–3
Real improvement visible
If you keep training, your MAF pace will be noticeably faster than week 1 at the same heart rate. Fat burning has improved. Easy runs feel genuinely easy. People start commenting that you look like a runner.
M4–6
Months 4–6
The transformation
Floris says this is where the aerobic base really locks in. Your easy pace may be 1–3 min/mile faster than it was at the start. You can run for an hour without getting winded. Speed sessions actually produce speed now — because the base is strong enough to absorb them.
Most people quit at week 3, right before the breakthrough. The runners who trust the process past that point are the ones who transform.
Floris Gierman — Extramilest Podcast
03
After race day
What comes after the 5K

The 5K is not the end — it's the first proof point. You've built a real aerobic base. Now the question is what you do with it. Most people who cross that finish line breathing easy want to keep going. Here's what the path forward looks like.

📅
Keep coming to Saturday club runs
The club run doesn't stop at week 8. Consistency over months is what turns a decent aerobic base into a strong one. Show up Saturdays.
🔄
Restart the plan at a higher level
Beginners can move to the intermediate plan. Intermediates can repeat the plan with higher volume easy runs. The base builds every cycle.
🏅
Set a new race goal
10K, half marathon, or a faster 5K — having a goal on the calendar keeps you consistent. Find a local race 8–12 weeks out and build toward it.
📊
Run a MAF progress test
Pick the same flat route you used in week 1. Run at exactly your MAF heart rate for 20–30 minutes and compare your pace. That gap is your 8 weeks of work.
📻
Dig into Floris's podcast
The Extramilest podcast goes deep on MAF training, nutrition, mindset, and long-term progression. Start with any episode on MAF training for beginners.
🤝
Bring a friend to Saturday
The hardest part of staying consistent is doing it alone. Every runner you bring to Saturday is one more person who finishes their 5K breathing easy.
The 5K wasn't the goal. The 5K was the proof. The real goal was building an engine that lasts.
