Why you get winded
You've been living in the "grey zone"
Most runners at this stage run every run at the same moderate pace — hard enough to be uncomfortable, not hard enough to produce real adaptations. Your aerobic base is underdeveloped. Your body hasn't built enough mitochondria to sustain effort efficiently, so it panics and reaches for anaerobic energy too early. The result: you're always slightly out of breath.
The fix — Floris Gierman & MAF Method
Slow way down to speed up
This plan is 80% easy Zone 2 running — slower than you think is useful. Use the MAF formula: 180 minus your age = your heart rate ceiling on every easy run. The remaining 20% is purposeful intensity. After 8 weeks your easy pace will feel like what your hard pace feels like today. Speed follows patience.

Floris's first rule: Your easy runs must be easy — embarrassingly easy. If your Thursday tempo is going to work, the easy days can't be medium. Calculate your MAF number (180 minus your age) and keep every easy and club run strictly under it. If you don't have a watch, use the talk test: full sentences the entire time, no exceptions.

Be flexible: Floris teaches that your training should adapt to your life — not fight it. If you had a stressful week, poor sleep, or it's a hot morning, your heart rate will be higher than usual at the same pace. On those days, slow down or take walk breaks without guilt. A slower run at the right zone is always better than a faster run in the wrong one.

Sleep is training: Floris talks about this constantly — 7–9 hours this week is non-negotiable. Mitochondrial growth, capillary expansion, and cardiac adaptations all happen during sleep and recovery, not during the run itself. Treat this week seriously. Many runners find their easy pace feels noticeably more controlled after a proper recovery week.

The MAF progress test: Run today's club run on a flat route at exactly your MAF heart rate and note your pace. Compare it to your week 1 club run. If you're covering more ground at the same heart rate — your aerobic engine is growing. Floris recommends doing this check every 4 weeks. Most people see clear improvement by now.

Peak week mindset: You may feel tired mid-week with both a quality session and a tempo. That's normal — trust the process. Floris's race day rule: Start 20–30% slower than you think you should. The crowd energy will tempt you to sprint — resist it. Your aerobic base means you have energy in reserve. Negative splits (finishing faster than you started) almost always feel better and produce better times. The sign this whole plan worked? You cross the finish line breathing — not gasping.
