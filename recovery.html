01
The most underrated training tool
Sleep is when you get fitter

Every run you do is a stimulus — a signal to your body to adapt. But the adaptation itself — new mitochondria, stronger heart, better fat burning — happens during sleep, not during the run. Floris Gierman talks about sleep more than almost any other recovery tool. It's free, it's available every night, and most runners completely undervalue it.

The goal is 7–9 hours every night, not just before a hard session. Consistently short sleep (under 6 hours) raises your resting heart rate, slows recovery, and makes your easy pace feel harder than it should — which pushes you out of Zone 2 without realising it.

Sleep is the single most effective thing you can do to enhance athletic recovery and improve performance — and it costs nothing.

🌙
Consistent bedtime
Going to bed and waking at the same time every day — even weekends — does more for recovery than any supplement. Your body's repair cycle runs on a clock.
📱
No screens before bed
Blue light suppresses melatonin for up to 2 hours. Put the phone down 30–60 minutes before sleep and your sleep quality improves measurably.
🌡️
Cool room
Your body temperature drops to initiate deep sleep. A slightly cooler room (around 65–68°F / 18–20°C) helps you fall asleep faster and stay in deep sleep longer.
☕
Cut caffeine by 2pm
Caffeine has a half-life of 5–7 hours. A coffee at 3pm still has half its caffeine in your system at 9pm, disrupting your sleep architecture even if you fall asleep fine.
02
Your body doesn't separate life from training
Stress is stress — your heart rate doesn't care where it comes from

This is one of Floris's most important lessons and one most running plans completely ignore. Your body treats work stress, relationship stress, financial stress, and training stress as the same thing. They all raise cortisol. They all elevate your resting heart rate. A brutal week at work followed by a hard Saturday run is not two separate events — it's a compounding load on the same body.

This is exactly why your MAF heart rate is so valuable. On a high-stress day, your heart rate will be higher than usual at the same pace. That's your body telling you something. Listen to it — slow down, take walk breaks, or rest entirely. It's not weakness. It's smart training.

Training should adapt to your life — not fight it. A slower run at the right heart rate is always worth more than a fast run in the wrong zone.
Floris Gierman — Extramilest Method
😤
Hard week at work
Cortisol is elevated. Your resting HR may be 5–10 bpm higher than normal. Slow your easy runs down to keep them under your MAF number. Skip the quality session if you're exhausted.
🤒
Feeling under the weather
If symptoms are above the neck (runny nose, mild headache), an easy Zone 2 run is usually fine. Symptoms below the neck (chest tightness, body aches, fever) — rest completely. Running through illness extends it.
🥵
Hot Bay Area morning
Heat raises your heart rate by 5–20 bpm at the same effort. On hot days, slow down significantly or move your run to early morning before the heat builds. Your pace will be slower — that's correct, not a failure.
😴
Poor night of sleep
One bad night won't ruin your training. A gentle Zone 2 run can actually help. But two or more bad nights in a row? Consider replacing a run with a walk or an extra rest day. Your body needs the recovery more than the mileage.
📅
Missed a session
Never try to "make up" a missed run by doubling up the next day. Just move on. One skipped session in an 8-week plan is completely insignificant. Doubling up creates injury risk and disrupts recovery.
03
Fuel for aerobic running
Eat to support your engine, not your ego

Zone 2 training runs primarily on fat as fuel — which is actually one of its biggest advantages. You have tens of thousands of calories of fat stored even as a lean runner, so you're unlikely to "bonk" on a 45-minute easy run. The nutrition focus for this club is simple: eat real food, stay hydrated, and don't overthink it.

Floris doesn't push complex nutrition protocols for beginners. His advice: eat mostly whole foods, don't run fasted if you feel bad doing it, and hydrate consistently throughout the day — not just before runs.

Topic
What helps
What to avoid
Before a run
Light snack 60–90 min before — banana, toast, oats. Something easy to digest.
Heavy meals within 1 hour — cramps, stitches, and sluggishness mid-run.
During a run
Water is fine for runs under 45 minutes. Sip if you're thirsty — don't force it.
Energy gels and sports drinks are unnecessary for Zone 2 runs under 60 minutes. They train your body to rely on sugar.
After a run
Protein within 30–60 min — eggs, Greek yogurt, chicken, beans. Kickstarts muscle repair.
Skipping the post-run meal — slows recovery and leaves you hungrier and more fatigued later.
Hydration
Drink consistently all day — not just before runs. Pale yellow urine is the target.
Chugging water right before — causes sloshing, discomfort, and doesn't actually hydrate you faster.
Everyday eating
Whole foods, vegetables, lean protein, healthy fats — supports fat adaptation and aerobic performance.
Ultra-processed foods and excess sugar — disrupt fat burning and blunt the Zone 2 adaptation signal.
You don't need to eat like an elite athlete. You need to eat like someone who respects the work they just put in.

04
Put it all together
Your weekly recovery checklist

You don't need to do all of this perfectly. But the more of these you consistently hit, the faster your aerobic base will grow and the better Saturday's club run will feel.

✓
Sleep 7–9 hours at least 5 nights this week — ideally every night. Prioritise this above any extra training.
✓
Check your resting heart rate first thing in the morning. If it's 5+ bpm above your normal, go easier or take a rest day.
✓
Drink water consistently throughout the day. Don't wait until you're thirsty before a run.
✓
Eat a protein-rich meal within 60 minutes after your longer runs and club sessions.
✓
Take rest days seriously. Monday rest is not optional — it's the day your body rebuilds from Saturday's club run.
✓
Slow down when life is stressful. Adjust your MAF pace on hard weeks. Speed follows patience — and patience includes knowing when to back off.
✓
Walk on your rest days if you feel like moving. A 20-minute walk keeps blood flowing without adding training stress.
✓
Show up to Saturday's club run. Consistency over 8 weeks beats any single perfect session. The community keeps you accountable — use it.
