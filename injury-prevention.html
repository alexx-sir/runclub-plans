Injury PreventionStay on the road. Stay consistent.

Most running injuries are preventable. This guide covers the most common ones, how to spot them early, and the exercises that keep them away.

🛡️
Zone 2 training is already your best injury prevention tool. Running at an easy, conversational pace means less impact stress, more time for tissue to adapt, and no grinding through fatigue that breaks form. The exercises below are your second layer of protection — do them 2–3x per week alongside your training plan.
Pre-Run Warmup Routine
Do this before every run. Takes 5–7 minutes. Never skip it — cold muscles are the most common reason for strains and pulls.
Leg swings
10 each direction, each leg
Stand on one leg, swing the other forward and back, then side to side. Opens the hip joint before running.
Hip circles
10 each direction
Hands on hips, draw large circles with your hips. Warms up the hip flexors and glutes.
Ankle rolls
10 each direction, each foot
Lift one foot and slowly roll the ankle through its full range of motion. Prevents ankle sprains and plantar fasciitis.
Walking lunges
10 steps each side
Step forward into a lunge, lower your back knee toward the ground. Activates glutes and stretches hip flexors simultaneously.
Calf raises
15 reps, slow
Rise up on your toes slowly, lower slowly. Warms up the Achilles tendon and calf complex before foot strike loading.
High knees (slow)
20 steps
Walk forward bringing each knee up to hip height. Gets blood moving into the quads and activates the core.

The Most Common
Running Injuries & Fixes
Runner's Knee (Patellofemoral Pain)
Knee
What it is & symptoms

Pain around or behind the kneecap, especially when going downhill, descending stairs, or sitting for long periods. Usually caused by weak glutes and quads forcing the kneecap to track incorrectly.

  • Dull ache around the front of the knee
  • Pain that gets worse going downstairs
  • Clicking or grinding sensation under the kneecap
  • Stiffness after sitting for a long time
Prevention exercises
Clamshells
3 sets × 15 reps each side
Lie on your side with knees bent, feet together. Keep feet touching and rotate your top knee up toward the ceiling like a clamshell opening. Slow and controlled.
Wall sit
3 sets × 30–45 seconds
Back flat against the wall, thighs parallel to the floor. Hold. Builds quad endurance which takes load off the kneecap.
Step-downs
3 sets × 12 reps each leg
Stand on a step, slowly lower the other foot toward the floor without touching, then raise back up. Controls the knee tracking pattern.
IT Band Syndrome
Knee / Hip
What it is & symptoms

Sharp, burning pain on the outside of the knee. The IT band is a thick strip of connective tissue running from your hip to your shin — when it gets tight, it rubs against the outer knee bone. Very common in new runners increasing mileage too fast.

  • Sharp pain on the outer side of the knee
  • Pain that starts about 10–15 minutes into a run
  • Sometimes a clicking feeling on the outside of the knee
  • Tight outer hip and thigh
Prevention exercises
Side-lying hip abduction
3 sets × 15 reps each side
Lie on your side, legs straight. Lift the top leg about 18 inches, hold for 2 seconds, lower slowly. Strengthens the glute med which controls IT band tension.
Figure-four stretch
Hold 45 seconds each side, 2–3 rounds
Lie on your back, cross one ankle over the opposite knee. Pull both legs toward your chest until you feel a stretch in your outer glute and hip.
Lateral band walks
3 sets × 15 steps each direction
Place a resistance band above your knees, slight squat position, walk sideways keeping constant tension on the band. Directly targets IT band weakness.
Plantar Fasciitis
Foot
What it is & symptoms

Inflammation of the thick band of tissue that runs along the bottom of your foot. The telltale sign is sharp heel pain first thing in the morning when you take your first steps. Caused by tight calves, weak foot muscles, or too much mileage too soon.

  • Sharp stabbing heel pain in the morning
  • Pain that improves after warming up but returns later
  • Aching in the arch after a run
  • Tenderness at the base of the heel when pressed
Prevention exercises
Towel toe scrunches
3 sets × 20 reps each foot
Lay a small towel flat on the floor. Use your toes to scrunch it toward you. Strengthens the intrinsic foot muscles that support the arch.
Calf stretches (wall)
Hold 45 seconds, 3 rounds each side
Place your hands on a wall, one foot back with the heel flat on the floor, lean forward until you feel the stretch up the back of the lower leg. Tight calves are the main driver of plantar fasciitis.
Frozen bottle roll
2 minutes each foot after runs
Sit in a chair and roll the arch of your foot over a frozen water bottle. Reduces inflammation and massages the fascia at the same time.
Shin Splints
Lower Leg
What it is & symptoms

Pain along the inner edge of the shinbone. Almost always caused by too much mileage too quickly, especially on hard surfaces. The muscles and connective tissue around the tibia become overloaded and inflamed.

  • Aching or throbbing along the front or inner shin
  • Pain that gets worse during a run
  • Mild swelling along the lower leg
  • Tenderness when you press along the shin bone
Prevention exercises
Toe taps
3 sets × 20 reps each foot
Sit in a chair, heel on the floor, tap your toes up and down as fast as you comfortably can. Strengthens the tibialis anterior — the muscle most involved in shin splints.
Eccentric calf raises
3 sets × 15 reps
Use both feet to rise up, then lower yourself back down on one foot only, very slowly (count 4 seconds down). The slow lowering builds tendon and muscle resilience.
Tibialis raise (wall)
3 sets × 15 reps
Stand with your back against a wall, heels about 6 inches out. Lift your toes off the floor keeping your heels down. Hold for 2 seconds at the top.
Achilles Tendinitis
Ankle
What it is & symptoms

Inflammation and pain in the Achilles tendon at the back of the ankle. Usually develops when runners increase pace or distance too quickly. The Achilles absorbs enormous force with every step — it needs time to adapt.

  • Stiffness and pain at the back of the ankle, especially in the morning
  • Tenderness when you pinch the tendon
  • Pain that eases as you warm up but returns after
  • Swelling or thickening of the tendon
Prevention exercises
Eccentric heel drops
3 sets × 15 reps each leg — daily
Stand on the edge of a step on the balls of your feet. Use both feet to rise up, then lower on one foot with your heel dropping below the step level. This is the gold-standard Achilles exercise.
Soleus stretch
Hold 45 seconds, 3 rounds each side
Similar to a calf stretch but bend your back knee slightly. This targets the soleus (the deeper calf muscle) which attaches directly to the Achilles.
Single-leg balance
3 × 30 seconds each leg
Stand on one leg. When that becomes easy, close your eyes. When that's easy, do it on a folded towel. Builds ankle stability and tendon control.
Sciatica / Piriformis Syndrome
Hip / Back
What it is & symptoms

Pain that radiates from the lower back or glute down through the leg. The sciatic nerve runs through the piriformis muscle in the deep glute — when that muscle is tight or weak, it can compress the nerve. Running aggravates but doesn't usually cause this.

  • Aching or burning in the glute that travels down the leg
  • Tingling or numbness down the back of the thigh
  • Worse when sitting for long periods
  • Pain on one side only, usually
Prevention exercises
Figure-four stretch (piriformis)
Hold 60 seconds, 3 rounds each side
Lie on your back, cross one ankle over the opposite knee. Pull both legs toward your chest until you feel a deep stretch in the outer glute. This directly releases the piriformis muscle.
Glute bridges
3 sets × 20 reps
Lie on your back with knees bent, feet flat. Push through your heels and lift your hips until your body forms a straight line. Squeeze your glutes at the top. Strong glutes reduce sciatic nerve compression.
Cat-cow stretch
10 slow cycles, twice daily
On hands and knees, alternate between arching your back toward the ceiling (cat) and letting it drop toward the floor (cow). Mobilises the lumbar spine and relieves nerve pressure.
Weekly Strength Routine
Do this 2–3 times per week on non-consecutive days. These exercises target the exact muscles that protect runners from injury — glutes, hips, calves, and core. Takes about 20 minutes.
Single-leg glute bridge
3 × 12 each leg
Same as a glute bridge but one foot lifted. Much harder. Directly targets the glute med and prevents almost every knee and hip injury.
Reverse lunges
3 × 10 each leg
Step backward into a lunge. Easier on the knees than forward lunges and builds quad and glute strength runners need.
Dead bug
3 × 8 each side, slow
Lie on your back, arms toward ceiling, knees at 90°. Slowly lower opposite arm and leg toward the floor without letting your lower back lift. Core stability prevents all running injuries.
Calf raises (eccentric)
3 × 15, 4 seconds down
Rise on two feet, lower on one foot very slowly. Protects the Achilles and plantar fascia — the two most common running injuries by far.
Side plank
3 × 30 seconds each side
Builds lateral core stability which keeps your hips level when running — preventing IT band syndrome and lower back pain.
Romanian deadlift (single leg)
3 × 10 each leg
Stand on one leg, hinge forward keeping your back flat until you feel your hamstring engage, then return upright. Bulletproofs the hamstring and glute complex.
The golden rule of injury prevention

"The best ability is availability. A slow runner who shows up every week beats a fast runner who's always injured."

→ Never increase mileage more than 10% per week
→ One rest day minimum per week
→ Pain is a signal, not a challenge
