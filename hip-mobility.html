Hip MobilityFor runners who want to move better.

Six exercises that open your hips, protect your lower back, and make every Zone 2 mile more efficient. Do these three times a week and your running changes.

Why it matters 90/90 Stretch Couch Stretch Pigeon Pose Hip CARs Cossack Squat PAILS / RAILS Routine
🔵
Tight hips are the hidden problem in most runners. When your hips can't move through their full range of motion, your lower back, knees, and IT band compensate with every single stride. Over thousands of steps that compensation becomes pain. These six exercises address the root cause — not the symptom.
01
Better stride mechanics
Full hip extension at push-off means more power per stride without more effort. Tight hip flexors shorten your stride and force your lower back to compensate — wasting energy at every step.
02
Lower back protection
Most lower back pain in runners comes from the lumbar spine compensating for restricted hips. Open hips mean the spine stays neutral and the deep stabilisers — not the back muscles — do their job.
03
Zone 2 efficiency
At Zone 2 pace you're running for 25 to 45 minutes. Every degree of restricted hip mobility costs you extra oxygen over that duration. Better mobility at easy pace directly lowers your heart rate at the same speed.
🫀
The MAF connection. Dr. Phil Maffetone consistently links movement quality to aerobic efficiency. A restricted hip pattern creates muscular tension that elevates heart rate at any given pace — meaning your MAF ceiling gets hit sooner than it should. Addressing hip mobility is a direct investment in your MAF pace improvement.
01
The Six Exercises
Hip Mobility Protocol
90/90 Hip Stretch
60–90 sec each side
What it is & muscles targeted

The most comprehensive hip stretch available. Simultaneously addresses internal and external hip rotation — the two directions most restricted in runners — in a single position. Named for the 90 degree angles both legs form.

Piriformis Hip external rotators Hip internal rotators Glute medius TFL
Step-by-step cues
1
Sit on the floor. Place your front leg with the shin parallel to the front of your mat — knee at 90 degrees. Place your back leg to the side with the shin pointing behind you — knee at 90 degrees.
2
Sit up as tall as possible. Don't let your torso collapse sideways — that's your hip forcing the compensating movement.
3
Keeping your torso tall, hinge forward from your hips over your front shin. You should feel a deep stretch in the outer hip and glute of the front leg.
4
Hold 60 to 90 seconds breathing deeply. With each exhale, relax further into the position. Switch sides and repeat.
Key cue: If your front hip lifts off the floor, you've gone too far forward. Find the depth where the hip stays grounded and work there.
Deep Lunge / Couch Stretch
60–90 sec each side
What it is & muscles targeted

Kelly Starrett calls this the most important stretch for anyone who sits during the day — which is every runner. Targets the hip flexors and quad in a way no other stretch can match. The couch version (with the back foot elevated) is significantly more intense than a standard lunge stretch.

Psoas Iliacus Rectus femoris Hip flexors
Step-by-step cues
1
Kneel in a lunge position with your back knee on the floor. Place the top of your back foot against a wall or couch behind you — shin vertical against the surface.
2
Squeeze the glute of your back leg hard. This is the most important cue — without the glute squeeze you won't get the deep hip flexor stretch, you'll just feel it in the quad.
3
Maintaining the glute squeeze, push your hips forward until you feel a deep stretch at the front of the back hip. Your torso stays upright.
4
Hold 60 to 90 seconds. The stretch should be intense but not painful. Breathe steadily throughout. Switch sides.
Progression: Start with the basic kneeling version before using the wall. The wall version is significantly harder — build up to it over 2 to 3 weeks.
Pigeon Pose
90 sec – 2 min each side
What it is & muscles targeted

A yoga-derived stretch that creates deep external rotation of the front hip while simultaneously lengthening the hip flexors of the back leg. One of the most effective single exercises for runners dealing with piriformis tightness and sciatic irritation from sitting and running.

Piriformis Deep external rotators Glute max Hip flexors (back leg) IT band (indirect)
Step-by-step cues
1
Start on all fours. Bring your right knee forward toward your right wrist, placing your right shin diagonally across the mat. Your right foot points toward your left hip.
2
Slide your left leg straight back behind you. The top of your left foot rests on the floor.
3
Slowly lower your hips toward the floor. If your right hip doesn't reach the floor, place a folded blanket or block underneath it — do not let your hips tilt to compensate.
4
Walk your hands forward and lower your torso toward the floor over your front shin. Hold 90 seconds to 2 minutes. Switch sides.
Easier version: Figure-four stretch on your back achieves the same effect with less intensity. Start there if pigeon pose feels too intense on the knee.
Hip CARs (Controlled Articular Rotations)
5 slow reps each direction, each side
What it is & muscles targeted

CARs are active mobility work — you're moving the joint through its full range under muscle control, not just stretching passively. Developed by Dr. Andreo Spina, they build active hip mobility that translates directly to running mechanics. Unlike stretching, CARs train your brain to actually use the range you have.

Full hip musculature Hip flexors Glutes (all) Deep rotators Adductors
Step-by-step cues
1
Stand on one leg holding a wall for balance. Brace your standing leg, glutes, and core — the rest of your body should not move throughout this exercise.
2
Slowly lift the free knee forward and up as high as possible. Then rotate the hip out to the side, keeping the knee as high as you can.
3
Continue the circle by moving the knee behind you, then under you and back to the start. This is one forward rotation.
4
Reverse the direction for the same number of reps. Do 5 rotations each direction, then switch legs. The entire movement should take 4 to 6 seconds per rotation — slow is the point.
Critical cue: The body must not rotate with the hip. If your pelvis tilts or your torso twists, reduce the range until you can keep everything still. Range means nothing if your body compensates for it.
Lateral Lunge / Cossack Squat
8–10 slow reps each side
What it is & muscles targeted

One of the most overlooked mobility exercises for runners. While most running movements are forward-back, the lateral lunge develops the side-to-side hip mobility that stabilises the pelvis with every footstrike. A key exercise from Jay Dicharry's biomechanics work on hip stability during running gait.

Adductors Hip abductors Glute medius Glute max Hip flexors Hamstrings
Step-by-step cues
1
Stand with feet wide — wider than shoulder width. Both feet pointing forward.
2
Shift your weight to one side, bending that knee and pushing the hip back as if sitting into a chair. The other leg stays straight with the foot flat or heel raised if flexibility is limited.
3
Hold at the bottom for 2 seconds. You should feel a stretch across the inner thigh of the straight leg and deep in the bent hip.
4
Push through the bent heel to return to centre, then shift to the other side. Alternate smoothly. Work toward keeping the heel of the straight leg on the floor.
Cossack version: In the full Cossack squat, the foot of the straight leg points upward — toes to ceiling. This intensifies the adductor stretch significantly. Work toward this gradually over weeks.
PAILS / RAILS (Progressive & Regressive Angular Isometric Loading)
2 min passive + 2 × 10 sec isometrics each side
What it is & muscles targeted

The most advanced technique in this protocol — developed by Dr. Andreo Spina as part of Functional Range Conditioning. PAILS/RAILS doesn't just stretch the hip — it builds active strength at the end ranges where running injuries occur. Done correctly after your passive stretches, it permanently expands usable hip mobility rather than just temporarily lengthening tissue.

Full hip musculature Deep rotators Glute complex Adductors Hip flexors
Advanced technique. Only attempt PAILS/RAILS after 4+ weeks of the passive stretches above. Your tissue needs preparation time before isometric loading at end range.
Step-by-step cues
1
Get into your 90/90 position. Spend 2 full minutes in passive stretch — breathe, relax, let gravity do the work.
2
PAILS (Progressive): Without moving, push the front shin and knee down into the floor as hard as you can — as if trying to close the hip angle. Build to maximum effort over 3 seconds, hold max effort for 7 seconds. This contracts the muscles being stretched.
3
Immediately release the PAILS contraction. Take one breath.
4
RAILS (Regressive): Now actively try to lift the front knee as high as possible — contracting the hip flexors to pull deeper into the range. Maximum effort for 10 seconds. This contracts the muscles on the opposite side.
5
Relax. You should now be able to move deeper into the 90/90 position than before. Repeat once more then switch sides.
Why it works: Passive stretching lengthens tissue temporarily. PAILS/RAILS creates neurological change — your brain learns the new range is safe and lets your muscles actually use it during running.

How to use it
Weekly Routine
Recommended schedule
3 sessions per week — takes 20 minutes
Do these on rest days or after easy runs — never before a run. Cold muscles stretch poorly and warm muscles stretch better. Post-run or post-shower is the optimal time.
