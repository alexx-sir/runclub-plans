The foundation of everything

Zone 2The most important run you'll ever do

Most runners skip Zone 2 because it feels too easy to be useful. That's exactly why most runners plateau. Here's what it is, why it works, and how to actually do it.

01
The basics
What is Zone 2?

Your heart rate exists on a spectrum from resting to maximum. Zone 2 is the second lowest intensity zone — a pace where your body is working aerobically, using oxygen efficiently to burn fat as its primary fuel source.

Think of your body as having two engines: an aerobic engine (efficient, uses fat and oxygen, can run for hours) and an anaerobic engine (powerful, burns glucose, burns out fast). Zone 2 trains your aerobic engine exclusively.

The key thing to understand: Zone 2 should feel almost embarrassingly easy. Most people run it too fast. If you feel like you could run significantly faster — you're probably in the right place.

02
The science (simplified)
Why does Zone 2 work?

Zone 2 training triggers a specific biological adaptation: your body builds more mitochondria — the tiny power plants inside your muscle cells. More mitochondria means more aerobic capacity, which means you can run faster and longer before getting winded.

The goal is to get to a point where a pace that once felt hard now feels easy — without having trained at that hard pace.
The principle behind Zone 2 training
⚡
More Mitochondria
Zone 2 is the primary stimulus for mitochondrial growth. More mitochondria = more aerobic power = less breathlessness at any given pace.
🔥
Fat Burning
At Zone 2, your body uses fat as its main fuel. Training this system means you have a near-unlimited energy source during runs, reducing fatigue.
❤️
Heart Strength
Easy aerobic running strengthens your cardiac output — how much blood your heart pumps per beat — so every organ and muscle gets more oxygen.
🛡️
Injury Prevention
Low intensity means low stress on joints and tendons. You can accumulate far more training time in Zone 2 without breaking down than at harder efforts.

Here's the paradox that trips everyone up: the less effort you put in during Zone 2 runs, the more adaptation happens. Pushing harder actually blunts the signal. Your body needs to stay in fat-burning mode — cross into Zone 3 and you've switched fuels and lost the stimulus.

03
The practice
How to actually run Zone 2

Zone 2 is defined by effort and heart rate — not speed. Your Zone 2 pace might be a 7 min/km jog, or it might be a brisk walk. Both are valid. Here's how to find and stay in it.

✓ The Talk Test — Your Built-In Sensor
✓
You can speak in full, complete sentences without gasping
✓
You feel like you could run significantly faster if you wanted to
✓
Your breathing is elevated but fully under control
✓
You could sustain this pace for a very long time
✗
If you're struggling to get words out — you've gone too fast, slow down
✗
If your sentences are broken up by breaths — dial it back
1
Find your Zone 2 heart rate
A simple formula: 180 minus your age. That's roughly the top of your Zone 2. For a 35-year-old, that's 145 bpm. Don't exceed this number during easy runs. If you don't have a heart rate monitor, the talk test works just as well.
2
Slow down — probably a lot
Most people need to slow down by 60–90 seconds per kilometre compared to what they think is easy. This feels uncomfortable at first. Embrace it. The pace will naturally speed up over weeks as your aerobic base develops.
3
Walk if you need to
If you can't hold a conversation at a run, walk until you can. Walking at a brisk pace is Zone 2. There is no shame in walking — it's the same aerobic stimulus. What matters is keeping your heart rate in range, not your feet moving.
4
Run by feel, not by pace
Ignore your pace on the watch. Hills, heat, and fatigue all change what pace corresponds to Zone 2. Run by effort and heart rate, not by trying to hit a speed. Some days Zone 2 is 6:00/km, some days it's 7:30/km — both are fine.
5
Do it consistently, over months
Zone 2 adaptations are slow and cumulative. You won't feel different after one week. After 6–8 weeks, your easy pace will feel noticeably smoother. After 3–6 months, the transformation is significant. Consistency is everything.

Common Zone 2 mistakes to avoid:

⚡
Running too fast
The most common mistake by far. If you're running at what feels like a "comfortable jog," you're probably in Zone 3. Zone 2 should feel almost too slow. Use the talk test religiously.
📅
Expecting quick results
Mitochondrial growth takes 4–8 weeks to show up. Many runners quit Zone 2 training before the adaptations kick in because it "doesn't feel like it's working." Trust the process.
🔀
Living in the grey zone
Running every session at the same moderate pace — too hard to be Zone 2, too easy to be a real quality session. This is the most common plateau. Easy runs must be easy. Hard runs must be hard.
🏔️
Not adjusting for hills
Going uphill sends your heart rate up fast. Slow to a walk on steep hills to stay in Zone 2 — this is not weakness, it's correct execution. Your effort stays the same; your pace changes.
